Abstract

Fine-grained perception and accurate spatial grounding remain persistent challenges for multimodal large language models (MLLMs). While prior work has focused on modifying model internals, we ask a different question: can we reshape the input image itself to better reflect what the model needs to see? We introduce Constructive Distortion, a simple, test-time image transformation that uses the model's own cross-modal attention to guide pixel-space warping. By expanding regions deemed important to the current query and compressing the rest, our method reallocates spatial resolution before the image is encoded‚Äîwithout any changes to model weights or architecture. This constructive distortion improves the visibility of fine details while preserving global layout. Across five benchmarks (TextVQA, GQA, DocVQA, POPE, MMMU), and two MLLMs (LLaVA, Qwen-VL), our method consistently improves accuracy, reduces hallucination, and enhances attention localization‚Äîoutperforming four strong test-time baselines.

Query-Aware Warping Results

Each example below shows how our method adaptively warps images based on different queries, highlighting relevant regions while preserving spatial relationships.

Query:

"On the right desk, what is to the left of the laptop?"
Warping focuses on the left side of the laptop on the right desk.

Query:

"What text appears on the computer screen?"
Screen regions are emphasized to improve text readability.

Query:

"How many people are visible in the image?"
Regions containing people are expanded for better counting.

Query:

"What is written on the sign in the background?"
Background sign text becomes more visible through warping.

Query:

"What color is the object on the table?"
Object regions are enhanced for better color identification.

Query:

"Is there a clock visible in the room?"
Warping highlights potential clock locations for detection.

Query:

"What brand is the laptop computer?"
Laptop logo/brand areas are magnified for identification.

Query:

"Are there any books on the shelf?"
Shelf regions are expanded to better identify book presence.
